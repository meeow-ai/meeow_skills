# Brand Discovery Methodology
## Source: Professional agency practice + RISD/Gretel/ON ROAD research process

---

## The Philosophy

> "Professional discovery is not a questionnaire. It is a structured diagnostic."
> — MalbarDesign, 2026

Real agency discovery (as practiced by Gretel for RISD, Pentagram for The New School,
and boutique studios worldwide) follows a pattern:

1. **Listen before you design.** The creative brief emerges from understanding, not assumption.
2. **Involve the community.** For a brand, the "community" is the client's life, taste, relationships, and ambitions.
3. **Find the common thread.** RISD's entire identity emerged from one phrase: "Question to create, create to question." One sentence, found through months of interviews.
4. **Strategy before aesthetics.** Color and font come last. Soul comes first.

The client who says "I want something clean and professional" doesn't know what they want yet.
Your job is to discover it through the right questions — not accept the placeholder.

---

## The Five Phases

---

### Phase 1 · Who Are You (Not What Do You Do)

**Goal:** Understand the person before the project. Brand is personality made visible.

**Time:** 5–10 exchanges. Warm, unhurried.

**Opening:**
> "Before we talk about the project — tell me about yourself. Not your job title.
> Just who are you? What do you do when nobody's watching?"

**Question bank — pull 3–5, follow the thread:**

- What's your superpower? What do people always come to you for?
- How do you describe what you do to your grandmother?
- What's the 3-year dream — not the realistic one, the real one?
- Is this a solo venture or do you have a team? Who are you trying to reach?
- What do you wish people understood about you that they usually don't?
- If your work could say one thing to the world, what would it be?

**Watch for:**
- The contradiction between how they describe themselves and what they actually care about
- Moments of unexpected passion — that's where the brand lives
- The "grandmother test" answer is often the purest version of the brand promise

**Red flag:** "I just want something clean and professional."
**Response:** "Clean like a hospital or clean like a Japanese restaurant? Tell me more."

---

### Phase 2 · The Space They Live In

**Goal:** Establish aesthetic DNA through lived experience, not design preferences.
Never ask "what colors do you like." Instead, find the environment they thrive in.

**Time:** 5–8 exchanges.

**The Space Questions:**
- "Walk me into your favourite space — a room, a café, a city corner. What does it look like? Smell like?"
- "Is your home more collected and layered, or more empty and deliberate?"
- "When you walk into a space and immediately feel 'yes, this is me' — what does it look like?"

**The Phone Test:**
- "Tell me about your phone home screen. Minimal with two apps, or organised chaos with personality?"
*(This reveals: control vs. warmth, function vs. expression)*

**The Coffee/Food Test:**
- "Where do you sit in a coffee shop? Bright window seat or dark corner booth?"
- "What's a meal that feels most like you?"
*(Espresso = precision, restraint. Baklava + espresso = contradiction = interesting brand.)*

**The Wardrobe Test:**
- "Name a brand you actually wear — not because it's cool, but because it just fits right."
- "Is there a brand you'd never wear? What's wrong with it?"
*(The dislike is as revealing as the like. "No Gucci" = hates things that try too hard.)*

**The Object Test:**
- "Do you wear a watch? What kind?"
*(A TAG Heuer person is different from an Apple Watch person. Both are different from no-watch.)*
- "Is there an object you own that you think is close to perfect? What makes it right?"

**Synthesis cue:** By end of Phase 2 you should be able to describe their aesthetic
in three words. If you can't, ask one more lateral question.

---

### Phase 3 · Cultural Taste Map

**Goal:** Map aesthetic references across art, music, film, literature, and icons.
This phase reveals rhythm, emotion, complexity, and depth.

**Time:** 5–8 exchanges. Let them geek out. This is where personality fully emerges.

**Music (most revealing):**
- "What's playing when you're in your best mood?"
- "What comes on when you're actually working hard at midnight?"
- "Is there a piece of music that feels most like you? Not your favourite — most like you."
*(Tchaikovsky + Bésame Mucho + Morricone = cinematic, grand emotion, intimate warmth.
Different from someone who says "lo-fi hip hop and Taylor Swift.")*

**Film/Directors:**
- "Last film that made you think 'this is exactly my aesthetic' — visually, not just the story?"
- "Is there a director whose work you'd recognize anywhere?"
*(Scorsese + Tornatore + Tarantino + Matrix = precise structure, nostalgic ache, hidden depth, philosophical cool)*

**Art:**
- "Any artist, era, or image you keep coming back to?"
- "Museum, gallery, street art — which pulls you in?"

**Books:**
- "What's on your nightstand?" *(Even if a cat is sleeping on it.)*
- "Last thing you read that changed how you thought?"

**Idols:**
- "One person — real or fictional — who you think just *gets it*. Style, attitude, energy, all of it."

**The Contradiction Check:**
After Phase 3, look for the productive contradictions:
- Classical music + action films = controlled exterior, explosive interior
- Luxury brands + Old Navy = quality without performance
- Cinema Paradiso + Tarantino = tender nostalgia + ruthless precision

**These contradictions ARE the brand.** Don't resolve them — amplify them.

---

### Phase 4 · The Project

**Goal:** Understand scope, audience, and ambition — in plain language.
Only ask these AFTER you know the person. The project makes much more sense with context.

**Time:** 3–5 exchanges.

**The Essence:**
- "What is this thing we're building? Describe it to someone who's never used a computer."
- "What should someone feel the first time they land on it?"
- "Who is it for? Paint me a picture of one specific person — not a demographic. A human."

**The Competitor/Reference Audit:**
- "Show me something you've seen online with the right energy — doesn't have to be in your industry."
- "Show me something that made you think 'god I hate this' — what's specifically wrong with it?"
*(The hate is always more specific and useful than the love.)*

**The Ambition Question:**
- "If this project is wildly successful in 3 years, what does that look like?"
- "Is this a single page for now, or is this the first room of a larger house?"

**The Declaration:**
- "If this project could say one thing to the world — one sentence — what would it be?"
*(This becomes the brand's heartbeat. Everything else serves it.)*

**Scope note:** Document both the immediate deliverable AND the future potential.
A landing page client who mentions "maybe eventually a subscription" is worth treating
as a SaaS client from day one.

---

### Phase 5 · Synthesis & Handoff

**Goal:** Confirm the read, then produce the brief.

**Step 1: The Verbal Reflection**
Speak it back before writing anything. Get confirmation.

> "Here's what I'm hearing: you want something that feels like [X] but moves like [Y].
> Clean surface, but with depth that rewards people who look closely.
> The vibe I'm getting is [one memorable sentence]. Does that feel right?"

Adjust until they say "yes, exactly." or something better emerges from their correction.

**Step 2: The Brand Memo** *(for the client — no jargon)*

```
# [Project Name] — Brand Identity Brief
### Prepared by: [Agency]
### Client: [Name]
### Date: [Date]

## Who They Are
[2–3 sentences. The person, not the project. What drives them.
What they want the world to understand about them.]

## The Brand Paradox
[The productive contradictions that make this brand interesting.
Table format works well: Surface vs. Underneath]

## The Feeling
[One evocative paragraph. What is the experience of landing on this site?
Write it like you're describing a place, not a product.]

## The Declaration
[One sentence. The brand's heartbeat. What it would say if it could speak.]

## Cultural Reference Map
[Music · Film · Fashion · Art · Food — the mood board in words]

## Visual Direction *(described in feelings, not specs)*
- Overall atmosphere: [describe]
- Color feeling: [warm/cool, dark/light, rich/restrained]
- Typography personality: [editorial? utilitarian? warm? cold?]
- Motion/pacing: [slow and cinematic? crisp and instant?]
- Detail philosophy: [hidden gems? brutally minimal? layered?]

## Voice & Tone
[How does this brand speak? 3–5 adjectives + one example sentence
that captures the register perfectly]

## What This Brand Is Never
[3–5 hard no's. As important as the yes's.
"Never Gucci. Never startup gradient purple. Never trying too hard."]

## Scope
[Current deliverable: X]
[Future potential: Y — design system should scale to accommodate]
```

**Step 3: Design Handoff**
See `references/design-handoff.md` for the technical CSS/token translation.

## Conversation Pacing Guide

| Phase | Exchanges | Energy |
|-------|-----------|--------|
| 1 · Who Are You | 5–10 | Warm, personal, zero agenda |
| 2 · The Space | 5–8 | Curious, lateral, follow the thread |
| 3 · Cultural Map | 5–8 | Let them geek out, match their enthusiasm |
| 4 · The Project | 3–5 | By now you know them, be efficient |
| 5 · Synthesis | 2–3 | Confirm, then write |

**Total:** 20–34 exchanges for a full session.
Compress aggressively if the client gives rich answers early.
Slow down and go more lateral if they're reticent.

## Special Client Types

**"I just want something nice"**
→ Skip Phase 4 entirely. Go deep on Phase 2. Find their aesthetic first.
The project brief will emerge naturally once you know their taste.

**"I know exactly what I want"**
→ Let them talk. Then ask the one question that reveals what they actually mean:
"Show me something you hate — and tell me why."

**The technical client who can't articulate feel**
→ Ask about non-digital things exclusively. Home, car, coffee ritual, childhood bedroom.
The aesthetic is always there. It just doesn't map to design vocabulary yet.

**The client who wants everything**
→ The Declaration question is your scalpel. Force one sentence.
Everything else falls from that constraint.

**The shy or uncertain client**
→ Start with the music question. It's almost always the easiest entry point
and the most revealing answer.

## The RISD Principle

RISD's entire identity emerged from one guiding idea found through speaking with
scores of students, faculty, staff and alumni: "Question to create, create to question."
Questioning the world around us is how we start any creative process. It is at the
core of who we are, how we communicate and how we look.

Your discovery session is the same process. One sentence. Found through listening.
That sentence becomes the brand's spine. Everything else hangs from it.

Don't rush to find it. It will appear.
